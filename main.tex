\section{Introduction}

Over the past two years, artificial intelligence has moved from a futuristic
concept to a core academic tool. According to the Pew Research Center (2024),
more than 63\% of American teenagers report using AI for schoolwork, and nearly
one in five uses it daily. The tools they reach for --- ChatGPT, Gemini,
Perplexity --- were built as general-purpose conversational agents. None were
designed for the specific cognitive needs of a middle schooler trying to
understand the chain rule for the first time.

I experienced this problem directly. In my own coursework (AP Calculus BC,
Multivariable Calculus, Linear Algebra, AP CS) and while tutoring younger
students, I kept seeing the same thing: a student would ask a chatbot to explain
mitosis or derivative notation, the model would produce a fluent but dense
six-paragraph response, the student would skim it, decide they were still
confused, and ask for it to be simpler. The next response was usually longer.
This happened over and over again. It's a design problem, not a capability
problem --- the model has the knowledge, it just doesn't know how to teach.

My earlier project Notes360.ai taught me a lot about how students actually
interact with AI tools and what they need from explanations. That experience,
combined with my tutoring work, convinced me that the bottleneck is not model
capability but design philosophy. SimplrAI is my attempt to get the design
right.

My research question is:

\begin{quote}\itshape
How can an AI-based academic explanation system be engineered to produce fast,
accurate, and grade-appropriate explanations that reduce cognitive load and
meaningfully improve student comprehension?
\end{quote}
