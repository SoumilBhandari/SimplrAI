\section{Introduction}

Over the past two years, artificial intelligence has moved from a futuristic
concept to a core academic tool. According to the Pew Research Center (2024),
more than 63\% of American teenagers report using AI for schoolwork, and nearly
one in five uses it daily. The tools they reach for---ChatGPT, Gemini,
Perplexity---were built as general-purpose conversational agents, optimized
for breadth, fluency, and general reasoning. None were designed for the specific
cognitive needs of a thirteen-year-old trying to understand the chain rule.

In my own coursework, and during the year I spent tutoring middle- and
high-school students, I encountered the same pattern repeatedly. A student
would ask a chatbot to explain mitosis or derivative notation. The model would
produce a fluent, dense, six-paragraph response written at a collegiate level.
The student would skim it, decide they were still confused, ask for simpler.
The next response would often be longer. This is a design problem.

This project asks:

\begin{quote}\itshape
How can an AI-based academic explanation system be engineered to produce fast,
accurate, and grade-appropriate explanations that reduce cognitive load and
meaningfully improve student comprehension?
\end{quote}

To answer it, I designed and built SimplrAI, an explanation system for students
from sixth grade through the first years of college, wrapping a frontier LLM in
three deliberate layers: a structured prompting layer, a grade-level adaptation
layer, and a minimal interface.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{AI tutoring systems}

Koedinger et al.\ (2022) found that AI tutors can support conceptual
understanding when explanations are appropriately structured. OpenAI (2023) and
Anthropic (2024) optimize for general helpfulness, not pedagogical clarity. Pew
Research (2024) confirmed rapid student adoption but warned about inconsistent
accuracy. Dwivedi et al.\ (2023) argue LLMs are epistemically unreliable for
learning.

\subsection{Cognitive load theory}

Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) holds that working memory can only process
limited novel information at once. Long, dense explanations increase extraneous
load (Kalyuga, 2009; Paas \& van Merri\"enboer, 2020). Chi and Wylie's ICAP
framework (2014) finds active-construction explanations outperform passive ones.

\subsection{Hallucination and factual reliability}

Maynez et al.\ (2020) document LLM hallucination. OpenAI (2023) shows prompting
strategy influences accuracy. Mitigations: structured prompting (Li et al.,
2023) and retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020).

\subsection{Reading-level adaptation and interface design}

Lexile Framework (MetaMetrics, 2019): comprehension falls when text exceeds
learner's threshold. Xu et al.\ (2023): LLMs default to college prose unless
constrained. Nielsen Norman Group (2023), Reich (2020), Quinn et al.\ (2023):
chunked, low-noise interfaces outperform dense text for K--12 learners.

\subsection{Synthesis and gaps}

Three gaps: no deployed system targets grade-appropriate explanation; structured
prompts unevaluated for student comprehension; interface effects understudied.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{The SimplrAI system}

SimplrAI is a React/Node/Express/Supabase web application at
\href{https://simplrai.tech}{\texttt{simplrai.tech}}. Prompting layer enforces
four-part output (definition, example, analogy, application). Retrieval layer
searches 4,300-document corpus (OpenStax, Khan Academy, CK-12). Interface layer
renders expandable cards with ``explain simpler'' button.

\subsection{Participants}

$N = 73$ (middle school $n = 24$; high school $n = 31$; college $n = 18$).
Ages 11--22. All minors with parental consent.

\subsection{Design}

Three-arm between-subjects: SimplrAI, ChatGPT (GPT-5), textbook control.
Counterbalanced subjects: Pre-Algebra, Biology, U.S.\ History.

\subsection{Procedure}

Pre-test (10 questions) $\to$ 20 min study $\to$ post-test (10 questions) $\to$
5-item Likert survey.

\subsection{Materials and measures}

Quiz items teacher-validated. Reading level: Flesch--Kincaid. Accuracy: two
experts, four-point rubric, $\kappa = 0.84$.
