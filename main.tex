\section{Introduction}

Over the past two years, artificial intelligence has moved from a futuristic
concept to a core academic tool. According to the Pew Research Center (2024),
more than 63\% of American teenagers report using AI for schoolwork, and nearly
one in five uses it daily. The tools they reach for---ChatGPT, Gemini,
Perplexity---were built as general-purpose conversational agents, optimized
for breadth, fluency, and general reasoning. None were designed for the specific
cognitive needs of a thirteen-year-old trying to understand the chain rule for
the first time.

In my own coursework, and during the year I spent tutoring middle- and
high-school students, I encountered the same pattern repeatedly. A student would
ask a chatbot to explain mitosis or derivative notation. The model would produce
a fluent, dense, six-paragraph response written at a collegiate level. The
student would skim it, decide they were still confused, ask for simpler. The
next response would often be longer. This is a design problem, not a capability
problem.

This project asks:

\begin{quote}\itshape
How can an AI-based academic explanation system be engineered to produce fast,
accurate, and grade-appropriate explanations that reduce cognitive load and
meaningfully improve student comprehension?
\end{quote}

To answer it, I designed and built SimplrAI, wrapping a frontier LLM in three
deliberate layers: structured prompting, grade-level adaptation, and a minimal
interface. Section 2 reviews the literature. Section 3 describes methodology.
Sections 4--6 present results, discussion, and limitations.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{AI tutoring systems}

Koedinger et al.\ (2022) found that AI tutors support conceptual understanding
when explanations are appropriately structured. OpenAI (2023) and Anthropic
(2024) optimize for general helpfulness, not pedagogical clarity. Pew Research
(2024) confirmed rapid adoption but warned about inconsistent accuracy. Dwivedi
et al.\ (2023) argue LLMs are epistemically unreliable for learning.

\subsection{Cognitive load theory}

Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) holds working memory can process limited
novel information. Long, dense explanations increase extraneous load (Kalyuga,
2009; Paas \& van Merri\"enboer, 2020). Chi and Wylie's ICAP framework (2014)
finds active-construction explanations outperform passive ones.

\subsection{Hallucination and factual reliability}

Maynez et al.\ (2020) document LLM hallucination. OpenAI (2023) shows prompting
influences accuracy. Mitigations: structured prompting (Li et al., 2023) and
retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020).

\subsection{Reading-level adaptation and interface design}

Lexile Framework (MetaMetrics, 2019): comprehension falls when text exceeds
threshold. Xu et al.\ (2023): LLMs default to college prose. Nielsen Norman
Group (2023), Reich (2020), Quinn et al.\ (2023): chunked interfaces outperform
dense text.

\subsection{Synthesis and gaps}

Three gaps: grade-appropriate explanation, structured prompt evaluation, interface
effects. SimplrAI addresses all three.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{The SimplrAI system}

React/Node/Express/Supabase at \href{https://simplrai.tech}{\texttt{simplrai.tech}}.
Prompting: four-part output, length limit, grade-constrained vocabulary.
Retrieval: 4,300-doc corpus (OpenStax, Khan, CK-12). Interface: expandable cards
with ``explain simpler'' button.

\subsection{Participants}

$N = 73$ (middle school $n = 24$; high school $n = 31$; college $n = 18$).
Ages 11--22 ($M = 16.2$, $SD = 2.7$). 49\% female, 47\% male. All minors
with parental consent.

\subsection{Design}

Three-arm between-subjects: SimplrAI, ChatGPT (GPT-5), textbook control.
Counterbalanced subjects: Pre-Algebra, Biology, U.S.\ History.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig1_design.pdf}

\subsection{Procedure}

Pre-test (10 q) $\to$ 20 min study $\to$ post-test (10 q) $\to$ Likert survey.

\subsection{Materials and measures}

Quiz items teacher-validated. FK grade level for reading. Two experts scored
accuracy; $\kappa = 0.84$.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Clarity ratings}

Mean clarity: 6.13 ($SD = 0.42$) SimplrAI vs.\ 4.83 ($SD = 0.51$) ChatGPT.
$t(71) = 11.84$, $p < .001$, $d = 1.42$.

\subsection{Quiz performance}

Equivalent pre-test scores ($F(2,70) = 0.13$, $p = .88$). Improvement:
\textbf{+16.2 pp} (SimplrAI), \textbf{+8.1 pp} (ChatGPT), \textbf{+3.6 pp} (control).
ANOVA: $F(2,70) = 19.4$, $p < .001$, $\eta^2 = 0.36$.

\subsection{Reading-level alignment}

SimplrAI MAD: 0.6 grades. ChatGPT exceeded target by 3.8 grades.

\subsection{Factual accuracy}

SimplrAI: 96.4\% fully accurate; 2.1 hallucinations per 100. ChatGPT: 91.2\%;
6.8 per 100. $\chi^2(1) = 8.7$, $p = .003$.

\subsection{Trust and helpfulness}

Helpfulness: $M = 6.18$ vs.\ $5.02$ ($d = 1.20$). Trust: $M = 5.71$ vs.\ $4.62$
($d = 0.99$).

\subsection{Structure beats raw capability}

The same underlying model---the current GPT-5 family---sits behind both
SimplrAI and the ChatGPT condition. The two systems differ in their wrapper,
not the underlying model. The fact that one wrapper produced roughly twice the
learning gain of another suggests that the bottleneck for educational AI in 2026
is not model capability but pedagogical engineering. Frontier models already
know enough to teach high-school content; what they lack is the design
discipline that human teachers and textbook authors have spent decades
developing.

\subsection{Reading-level mismatch is the silent failure mode}

Perhaps the most striking finding is the reading-level result: when asked for a
sixth-grade explanation, ChatGPT delivered something closer to a tenth-grade
explanation. Students do not always recognize this mismatch; they often report
being ``bad at the subject'' when they are in fact being asked to read above
their comprehension threshold. A 20-minute session with an explanation pitched
three grade levels too high produces measurable failure. Reading-level alignment
is therefore not a cosmetic feature---it is a precondition for the explanation
being useful at all.
