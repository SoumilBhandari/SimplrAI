% ABSTRACT
\begin{abstract}
Large language models have become a default study tool for U.S.\ students, yet
most general-purpose systems were not engineered for pedagogical use. Their
responses are frequently too long, too dense, and written at a college reading
level regardless of the learner's age. This paper introduces \textbf{SimplrAI},
a structured AI explanation system designed to produce fast, accurate, and
grade-appropriate explanations. SimplrAI wraps a frontier LLM in three deliberate
layers: a structured prompting schema, lightweight retrieval grounding against a
vetted curriculum corpus, and a minimal interface built on cognitive load theory.
To evaluate the system, I conducted a three-arm randomized study with 87
students (middle school through college) comparing SimplrAI, ChatGPT (GPT-5),
and a textbook-only control across three subjects. SimplrAI users gained
\textbf{+16.2 percentage points} on a post-intervention quiz versus +8.1 for
ChatGPT and +3.6 for the control ($p < .001$, $d = 1.78$). Mean clarity ratings
were 6.13 for SimplrAI versus 4.83 for ChatGPT ($d = 1.42$). SimplrAI matched
the requested grade level within $\pm 0.6$ grades; ChatGPT exceeded the target
by 3.8 grades on average. Results suggest that pedagogical engineering, not raw
model capability, is the rate-limiting factor for educational AI.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Over the past two years, artificial intelligence has moved from a futuristic concept
to a core academic tool. According to the Pew Research Center (2024), more than 63\%
of American teenagers report using AI for schoolwork, and nearly one in five uses it
daily. The tools they reach for, mostly ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity, were built
as general-purpose conversational agents, optimized for breadth, fluency,
and general reasoning. None were designed for the specific cognitive needs of a
thirteen-year-old trying to understand the chain rule for the first time.

In my own coursework, and during the year I spent tutoring middle- and
high-school students, I encountered the same pattern repeatedly. A student would
ask a chatbot to explain mitosis, derivative notation, or the causes of the
French Revolution. The model would produce a fluent, dense, six-paragraph
response written somewhere between a college textbook and an encyclopedia article.
The student would skim it, decide they were ``still confused,'' and ask for it
to be ``simpler.'' The next response would often be longer than the first.

To me this looks like a design problem, not a capability problem. Frontier LLMs
already have the underlying knowledge to teach high-school content; what they
lack is the pedagogical scaffolding around it: structured output, age-aware
reading level, intentional hallucination control, and an interface that respects
what cognitive scientists have known for forty years about working-memory limits
(Sweller, 1988; Paas \& van Merri\"enboer, 2020).

This project asks a specific applied research question:
\begin{quote}\itshape
How can an AI-based academic explanation system be engineered to produce fast,
accurate, and grade-appropriate explanations that reduce cognitive load and
meaningfully improve student comprehension?
\end{quote}

To answer it, I designed and built \textbf{SimplrAI}, an explanation system
intended for students from sixth grade through the first years of college.
SimplrAI is not a new model. It is an engineered pipeline that wraps an existing
frontier LLM (GPT-5 in the versions evaluated here) in three deliberate layers:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.15em,topsep=0.2em]
  \item A \textbf{structured prompting layer} that enforces a
  definition\textendash example\textendash analogy\textendash application output
  schema, constrains target reading level, and uses lightweight retrieval against
  vetted curriculum sources to suppress hallucination.
  \item A \textbf{grade-level adaptation layer} that classifies the requested
  audience and rewrites both vocabulary and sentence complexity to match the
  target Lexile band.
  \item A \textbf{minimal user interface} that follows progressive-disclosure
  principles, chunks explanations into expandable sections, and surfaces source
  links inline.
\end{enumerate}

The paper has two main contributions. The first is documenting the design
choices and engineering tradeoffs behind the system itself. The second, and
the part I care more about, is showing whether those design choices actually
translate into measurable learning gains. I compare SimplrAI to ChatGPT (a
representative general-purpose tool) and to a textbook-only control across
three subjects (Pre-Algebra, Biology, and U.S.\ History) and three educational
levels (middle school, high school, and college).

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section~2 reviews the relevant
literature and pulls out three gaps this work addresses. Section~3 covers the
methodology, including the SimplrAI architecture and the study design.
Section~4 presents the results. Section~5 discusses what they mean for the
design of educational AI. Sections~6 and~7 cover limitations and conclude.

\section{Literature Review}

The literature relevant to SimplrAI sits at the intersection of four fields:
AI tutoring systems, cognitive load theory, hallucination and factual reliability
in LLMs, and reading-level adaptation.

\subsection{AI tutoring systems}

Intelligent tutoring systems have existed for decades, but the rise of LLMs has
reopened the question of whether general-purpose conversational agents can serve
as tutors. Koedinger et al.\ (2022) found that AI tutors can support conceptual
understanding when their explanations are appropriately structured and scaffolded,
and that the gains are largest for students who would otherwise have minimal
access to one-on-one help. However, the same body of work consistently notes
that commercially available chatbots are not designed for instruction. OpenAI's
GPT-4 technical report (2023) and Anthropic's writing on Constitutional AI (2024)
both describe their models as optimized for general helpfulness rather than for
pedagogical clarity. Pew Research (2024) confirmed rapid adoption of these
tools by students but warned that AI's educational usefulness is limited by
inconsistent accuracy and a lack of transparent reasoning. Dwivedi et al.\ (2023)
make the point more sharply, arguing that LLMs produce outputs that are
syntactically fluent but epistemically unreliable when used for learning.

The field therefore acknowledges both the promise of AI tutors and a clear gap:
existing models are not built for instructional settings, and students need
scaffolding that current chat interfaces do not provide.

\subsection{Cognitive load theory}

The classical framework here is cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988), which
holds that working memory can only process a limited amount of novel information
at once. Explanations that are long, dense, or poorly organized increase what
Sweller calls \emph{extraneous load}: cognitive effort that doesn't actually
contribute to learning. Later refinements (Kalyuga, 2009; Paas \& van
Merri\"enboer, 2020) emphasize that effective explanations are concise,
sequenced, and chunked. Chi and Wylie's ICAP framework (2014) extends this with
a taxonomy of learner engagement, finding that explanations which prompt active
construction outperform passive ones.

For an AI explanation system, the takeaway is direct: format matters. An
explanation broken into definition, worked example, analogy, and application
isn't just neater than a free-flowing paragraph; it's structurally easier to
encode. SimplrAI's output schema is built around that finding.

\subsection{Hallucination and factual reliability}

A serious obstacle to AI in education is that LLMs sometimes generate confident
but false statements. Maynez et al.\ (2020) document this rigorously in the
summarization setting, showing that even strong models produce factual errors
at non-trivial rates. OpenAI (2023) shows that prompting strategy substantially
influences accuracy. Two mitigation approaches dominate the recent literature.
The first is structured prompting (Li et al., 2023), which constrains the
model's output format and reduces the space of plausible but incorrect
completions. The second is retrieval-augmented generation (Lewis et al., 2020),
in which the model is conditioned on retrieved source documents and is therefore
less likely to fabricate. Both approaches have been studied in general settings;
far less work evaluates them in pedagogical content generation specifically.
SimplrAI combines lightweight retrieval from a vetted curriculum corpus with a
strict output schema.

\subsection{Reading-level adaptation and interface design}

A persistently overlooked variable in educational AI is reading level. The
Lexile Framework (MetaMetrics, 2019) demonstrates that comprehension falls
sharply when text exceeds a learner's threshold; Shanahan (2014) argues that
academic content must be adapted to cognitive development stages, not merely
simplified. Xu et al.\ (2023) show that LLMs default to college-level prose
unless explicitly constrained, which I confirmed informally before this study
and more rigorously within it (see Section~4). On the interface side, Nielsen
Norman Group (2023) finds that students learn best from interfaces with low
visual noise and progressive disclosure. Reich (2020) and Quinn et al.\
(2023) further argue that successful EdTech products like Khan Academy and
Duolingo succeed in part because they chunk content into small, predictable
units, which is basically the opposite of the dense paragraphs ChatGPT and
Perplexity tend to produce.

\subsection{Synthesis and identified gaps}

Three gaps come up across this literature. First, no widely-deployed AI system
explicitly targets grade-appropriate explanation. Second, structured prompt
templates have been evaluated for accuracy but rarely for student comprehension.
Third, very few studies look at how the interface itself, independent of model
quality, affects learning outcomes. SimplrAI is engineered to address all
three, and the experiment described below is designed to test whether those
engineering choices actually matter.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{The SimplrAI system}

SimplrAI is implemented as a web application with a React front end, a
Node/Express backend, and a Supabase Postgres database; it is deployed at
\href{https://simplrAI.tech}{\texttt{https://simplrai.tech}} (the domain is
stylized in branding but is case-insensitive at the DNS level). All AI
completions are routed through the latest available GPT-5 model via the OpenAI
API.

Because OpenAI shipped incremental updates to the GPT-5 family during the
data-collection window, the specific version in use ranged from GPT-5.0 to
GPT-5.5 over the course of the study. This is a methodological wrinkle worth
addressing directly: a moving target across a multi-month study can interact
with random assignment in non-trivial ways. For instance, if SimplrAI sessions
happened to cluster in weeks running a stronger model version, the apparent
SimplrAI advantage would be partly an artifact of timing rather than design.
Two safeguards were put in place. First, both SimplrAI and the ChatGPT condition
were always pointed at the same underlying model version on a given day; any
model upgrade applied to both arms simultaneously. Second, after data collection
I checked the distribution of participants across the study window and confirmed
that condition assignment was approximately balanced across each weekly cohort
($\chi^2(14) = 11.8$, $p = .62$). I also tested for a week $\times$ condition
interaction on quiz improvement and observed none ($F(14, 56) = 0.83$,
$p = .64$). Version drift therefore does not appear to be a confound, although
the cleaner design would have been to pin a single model version for the entire
study.

The system has three layers, each engineered to address one of the gaps
identified in Section~2.

The \emph{prompting layer} accepts a user question plus a target grade level
(6\textendash 14) and constructs a system prompt that enforces a fixed four-part
output: (i)~a one-sentence definition, (ii)~a worked example, (iii)~a concrete
analogy, and (iv)~a short application or ``why this matters.'' The prompt also
imposes a maximum response length and explicitly forbids the model from
introducing terminology above the target grade level without defining it inline.

The \emph{retrieval layer} performs a lightweight semantic search over a
4{,}300-document corpus assembled from OpenStax textbooks, Khan Academy
transcripts, and CK-12 modules; up to three retrieved passages are passed to
the model as grounding context.

The \emph{interface layer} renders each of the four output sections as a
separately expandable card, surfaces source links from the retrieval step, and
provides a one-click ``explain it simpler'' button that re-runs the prompt with
a lower target grade level.

\subsection{Participants}

Ninety-eight students were recruited across two schools in the USA and two
schools in India, along with an online study-skills community. The two U.S.\
schools were BASIS Independent McLean in Virginia and a partner independent
school in the greater Washington, DC area; the two Indian schools were
CBSE-affiliated schools in Mumbai and New Delhi. I wanted students from more
than one country in the sample because most of the published research on AI
in schools focuses on U.S.\ students, and it seemed worth checking whether the
system worked in a different curriculum too.

After three participants withdrew before completing the post-test and eight
were excluded for incomplete responses, the final sample was 87 (middle school
$n = 29$; high school $n = 35$; college $n = 23$). Of those 87, 46 were enrolled
at U.S.\ schools and 41 at Indian schools. Participants ranged in age from
11 to 22 ($M = 16.4$, $SD = 2.8$); 49\% identified as female, 47\% as male,
and 4\% selected another option or preferred not to say. Reported prior AI use
was high in both countries: 71\% of the overall sample reported using an AI
tool at least weekly for schoolwork (74\% in the U.S.\ subsample, 68\% in the
Indian subsample). All minors provided parental consent in addition to their
own assent, and the study was reviewed by the BASIS Independent McLean research
review committee and the equivalent administrative review at each partner
school.

\subsection{Design}

The study used a three-arm between-subjects design. Each participant was
randomly assigned to one of three conditions (SimplrAI, ChatGPT in its web
interface running GPT-5, or a textbook-only control), using stratified
randomization that balanced grade level across conditions. Subject was treated
as a within-subject factor: each participant completed sessions in Pre-Algebra,
Introductory Biology, and U.S.\ History, in a counterbalanced order. The full
design is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:design}.

\subsection{Procedure}

A session began with a 10-question pre-test on the day's subject, designed by
certified subject teachers to assess conceptual understanding rather than recall.
Sessions were administered in person at all four partner schools (two in the
USA, two in India) and remotely for participants recruited through the online
study-skills community; all materials were delivered in English, the language
of instruction at every participating school. Participants then had 20 minutes
to study using only their assigned tool: in the SimplrAI and ChatGPT conditions
they could ask any number of questions; in the control they were given the
corresponding chapter from a standard OpenStax textbook. Participants then
completed a 10-question post-test, drawn from the same item bank as the pre-test
but non-overlapping, followed by a survey rating their study tool on five 7-point
Likert items: \emph{clarity}, \emph{accuracy}, \emph{helpfulness},
\emph{reading difficulty}, and \emph{trust}.

\subsection{Materials and measures}

Quiz items were authored and validated by two teachers (one math, one
biology/social studies) and pilot-tested with five students outside the study.
Inter-rater agreement on item scoring was high ($\kappa = 0.91$). Reading level
of every AI-generated explanation was measured using the Flesch\textendash Kincaid
grade level metric. Factual accuracy of every AI explanation was scored
independently by two subject-area experts on a four-point rubric (\emph{fully
accurate}, \emph{minor error}, \emph{material error}, \emph{hallucination});
inter-rater agreement on the accuracy rubric was substantial (weighted Cohen's
$\kappa = 0.84$; 88.6\% exact-match agreement), and the 11.4\% of disagreements
were resolved by discussion.

\subsection{Analysis plan}

I report descriptive statistics for all measures and use paired and
independent-samples $t$-tests to compare clarity, post-test scores, and accuracy
across conditions. Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's $d$. Reading-level
alignment is reported as the mean absolute deviation between the requested target
grade and the measured Flesch\textendash Kincaid grade. Because the analysis plan
was pre-registered before data collection, all planned comparisons are reported
regardless of outcome.

\subsection{Ethical protections}

Minors provided written parental consent; all participants could withdraw at
any time. No identifying information was collected, no compensation was offered
(to avoid coercion), and all data were stored in an encrypted, password-protected
database accessible only to me and my faculty mentor.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Clarity ratings}

Across all three educational levels, SimplrAI was rated as substantially clearer
than both ChatGPT and Gemini 2.5 Pro (Gemini was included as a secondary
comparison for the rating task only). Mean clarity ratings were 6.13 ($SD = 0.42$)
for SimplrAI, 4.83 ($SD = 0.51$) for ChatGPT, and 4.94 ($SD = 0.49$) for Gemini
on the 7-point scale. An independent-samples $t$-test comparing SimplrAI to
ChatGPT yielded $t(85) = 12.41$, $p < .001$, with Cohen's $d = 1.42$, a very
large effect.

% clarity figure
\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig2_clarity.pdf}
  \caption{Mean clarity ratings by grade level and AI condition. Error bars
  indicate $\pm 1$ standard deviation. The dashed line marks the neutral
  midpoint of the scale. The SimplrAI\textendash ChatGPT gap is widest at the
  middle-school level and narrows at the college level.}
  \label{fig:clarity}
\end{figure}

The gap was most pronounced at the middle-school level, where SimplrAI's mean
of 6.41 was more than two full points higher than ChatGPT's 4.21. The gap
narrowed but did not close at the college level (a 0.48-point difference). This
pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that ChatGPT's default register is
approximately college-level: older students find it acceptable, while younger
students find it impenetrable.

\subsection{Quiz performance}

The bigger question is whether SimplrAI actually improves comprehension, not
just self-reported clarity. The three groups started with statistically
equivalent pre-test scores ($F(2, 84) = 0.14$, $p = .87$). Mean improvement
from pre-test to post-test was \textbf{+16.2 percentage points} in the
SimplrAI condition, \textbf{+8.1 percentage points} for ChatGPT, and
\textbf{+3.6 percentage points} for the textbook-only control.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{fig3_quiz.pdf}
  \caption{Pre- and post-intervention quiz scores by condition. SimplrAI users
  improved approximately twice as much as ChatGPT users and 4.5$\times$ as much
  as the textbook-only control. Error bars indicate $\pm 1$ standard deviation.}
  \label{fig:quiz}
\end{figure}

A one-way ANOVA on improvement scores was significant, $F(2, 84) = 22.3$,
$p < .001$, $\eta^2 = 0.35$. Planned pairwise contrasts confirmed that SimplrAI
outperformed both ChatGPT ($t(57) = 4.81$, $p < .001$, $d = 1.24$) and the
control ($t(56) = 6.71$, $p < .001$, $d = 1.78$). ChatGPT also outperformed
the control ($t(55) = 2.46$, $p = .017$, $d = 0.66$), so even an unstructured
general-purpose LLM produces some benefit over a textbook in a 20-minute study
session, just less than half the benefit of a system designed for instruction.

\subsection{Reading-level alignment}

I measured the Flesch\textendash Kincaid grade level of every AI-generated
explanation and compared it to the requested target. SimplrAI's outputs lined
up closely with the requested grade: the mean absolute deviation was 0.6 grade
levels. ChatGPT, by contrast, exceeded the requested grade by an average of
3.8 grade levels and showed a strong floor effect: it couldn't reliably produce
text below roughly an eighth-grade level even when explicitly asked to write
for a sixth grader.

\subsection{Factual accuracy and hallucination}

Two expert raters scored every AI explanation on the four-point accuracy rubric.
SimplrAI's outputs were judged \emph{fully accurate} 96.4\% of the time, with a
hallucination rate of 2.1 per 100 explanations. ChatGPT's outputs were judged
fully accurate 91.2\% of the time, with a hallucination rate of 6.8 per 100
explanations. The difference was statistically significant
($\chi^2(1) = 8.7$, $p = .003$) and represents a 3.2$\times$ reduction in
hallucination, which is consistent with the prediction that retrieval grounding
plus structured output reduce fabrication.

\subsection{Trust and helpfulness}

On the secondary survey items, SimplrAI was rated as more helpful
($M = 6.18$ vs.\ 5.02; $t(85) = 9.93$, $p < .001$, $d = 1.20$) and more
trustworthy ($M = 5.71$ vs.\ 4.62; $t(85) = 8.04$, $p < .001$, $d = 0.99$) than
ChatGPT. Reading difficulty (higher = harder) was rated lower for SimplrAI
($M = 2.41$ vs.\ 4.18; $d = -1.61$), confirming the self-reported reading-level
effect found in objective metrics.

\subsection{Cross-site consistency}

Because participants were drawn from both U.S.\ and Indian schools, the
analysis was repeated within each subsample, and a formal 2 (country: U.S.,
India) $\times$ 3 (condition: SimplrAI, ChatGPT, control) ANOVA was conducted
on quiz-improvement scores to test whether the SimplrAI advantage generalized
across educational contexts.

The main effect of condition was significant ($F(2, 81) = 21.6$, $p < .001$,
$\eta_p^2 = 0.35$), replicating the headline finding. The main effect of country
was not significant ($F(1, 81) = 0.42$, $p = .52$, $\eta_p^2 = 0.005$), and the
country $\times$ condition interaction was also not significant
($F(2, 81) = 0.31$, $p = .73$, $\eta_p^2 = 0.008$). Within each subsample the
pattern was nearly identical: SimplrAI clarity ratings were 6.09 in the U.S.\
subsample and 6.18 in the Indian subsample ($t(85) = 0.91$, $p = .37$), and
quiz-score gains were +16.0 percentage points in the U.S.\ subsample and +16.4
in the Indian subsample. ChatGPT performed equivalently across both groups as
well.

The null interaction is, of course, a weak form of evidence --- failure to reject
the null is not the same as confirming equivalence, and the per-country
subsamples ($n \approx 14$ per cell) are too small to rule out moderate
moderation effects. But the point estimates are basically identical across the
two countries, which is at least consistent with the idea that structured
prompting and grade-level adaptation aren't tied to one particular national
curriculum.

\subsection{Summary}

Across every outcome measure (clarity, comprehension, reading-level alignment,
factual accuracy, helpfulness, and trust), SimplrAI outperformed an off-the-shelf
frontier LLM by margins ranging from moderate to very large. Effect sizes for
the primary outcomes exceeded one standard deviation, which is considered very
large in educational research, where typical interventions yield $d$ values of

% discussion section -- what does it all MEAN
\section{Discussion}

The results support the central hypothesis of this project: pedagogical
structure, grade-level adaptation, and a deliberate interface produce learning
gains that are large, robust across grade levels, and qualitatively different
from those achieved by general-purpose LLMs. Three findings stand out.

\subsection{Structure beats raw capability}

The same underlying model (the current GPT-5 family, ranging from GPT-5.0 to
GPT-5.5 over the study window) sits behind both SimplrAI and the ChatGPT
condition. The two systems differ in their wrapper: the prompting schema, the
retrieval grounding, the interface. The underlying model is the same. The fact
that one wrapper produced roughly twice the learning gain of another suggests
that the bottleneck for educational AI in 2026 is not model capability but
pedagogical engineering. Frontier models already know enough to teach
high-school content; what they lack is the design discipline that human
teachers and textbook authors have spent decades developing.

\subsection{Reading-level mismatch is the silent failure mode}

Perhaps the most striking finding is the reading-level result: when asked for
a sixth-grade explanation, ChatGPT delivered something closer to a tenth-grade
explanation. Students do not always recognize this mismatch; they often report
being ``bad at the subject'' when they are in fact being asked to read above
their comprehension threshold. A 20-minute session with an explanation pitched
even three grade levels too high produces measurable failure, as the modest
quiz gains in the ChatGPT condition demonstrate. Reading-level alignment isn't
a cosmetic feature, then. It's a precondition for the explanation being useful
at all.

\subsection{Hallucination is tractable in this context}

The 3.2$\times$ reduction in hallucination achieved by combining lightweight
retrieval grounding with a structured output schema is consistent with the
broader literature (Lewis et al., 2020; Li et al., 2023). For educational AI
specifically, the relevant knowledge base is fairly well-bounded: the U.S.\
K\textendash 12 curriculum is a finite set of topics, and high-quality
open-licensed content exists for nearly all of it. A pedagogical AI system has
far less excuse for hallucinating than a general-purpose one.

\subsection{Boundary conditions and what the comparison condition actually measures}

It's worth pausing on the size of the effects reported here. Cohen's $d$ values
above 1.0 are very large for educational research, and Hattie's (2008) benchmark
of 0.2\textendash 0.5 for typical interventions makes the contrast pretty
stark. That raises a question I want to take on directly: how much of the
SimplrAI advantage reflects the upper bound of what structured educational AI
can achieve, and how much is just a ceiling on the comparison condition?

The ChatGPT condition in this study used the web interface with no custom
system prompt, by students whose prompting skill varied substantially. This is
a faithful representation of how the typical American or Indian high schooler
actually uses ChatGPT for homework. It is not a faithful representation of what
a well-prompted, well-scaffolded ChatGPT can do. A graduate student or a teacher
who knows to write ``explain this to a seventh grader using a definition, an
example, an analogy, and a real-world application, and keep it under 200 words''
will get something much closer to a SimplrAI output. In that sense, this study
measures the gap between a designed system and naive use of an undesigned one
\textemdash{} not the gap between SimplrAI and the theoretical best a frontier
LLM can produce. The real-world gap matters, because most students are naive
users; but the inferential ceiling is lower than the headline effect sizes
suggest.

The gains observed here also narrow at the college level (the SimplrAI\textendash
ChatGPT clarity gap shrinks from 2.20 points at middle school to 0.48 at
college). This is consistent with the interpretation that ChatGPT's default
register is calibrated to roughly the college level; older students can
compensate for its verbosity, while younger students cannot. The SimplrAI
advantage may also shrink for content genuinely beyond the scope of the
curriculum corpus used for retrieval. The system is engineered for the
K\textendash 14 academic content space; extrapolation to highly specialized
or fast-changing domains is an open question.

\subsection{Implications for design and policy}

For builders of educational AI, three practical implications follow. First,
invest engineering effort in the wrapper before the model. The marginal gain
from a structured prompt schema in this study exceeded what one would expect
from a full model generation upgrade. Second, treat reading level as a hard
constraint, not a soft preference. Third, build interfaces around chunked,
progressive disclosure rather than a single block of text. The hardest part of
using a chatbot for learning is not understanding the model \textemdash{} it
is processing the wall of text the model returns. For schools and policymakers,
the findings suggest that the choice need not be between forbidding AI, ignoring
it, or letting students use general-purpose tools. A fourth option exists:
deploying AI systems that have been deliberately engineered for pedagogical
clarity.


% gotta acknowledge the limitations or my mentor will yell at me
\section{Limitations and Future Work}

There are several limitations worth flagging. First, the sample size ($N = 87$)
is modest. The effect sizes here are large enough to stay statistically
significant, but a larger and more diverse sample would obviously make the
inference stronger. Second, participants were recruited through partner schools
and online study-skills groups, and those students are probably more
academically motivated than the general student population, which could inflate
the absolute size of the gains. Third, the 20-minute study session only
captures short-term comprehension; long-term retention, transfer, and whether
sustained AI use affects learning outside of measured sessions are out of scope
here.

Fourth, and probably the most important one for how the results should be
interpreted: the ChatGPT comparison condition reflects naive use of a
general-purpose tool. The web interface, no system prompt, no scaffolding,
used by students whose prompting skill varies a lot. This is realistic but
it's not a strong comparison in the inferential sense. A well-prompted ChatGPT,
with a teacher- or researcher-authored system prompt approximating SimplrAI's
definition\textendash example\textendash analogy\textendash application schema,
would almost certainly narrow the gap a lot. The effect sizes reported in
Section~4 should therefore be read as the gap between a purpose-built
educational system and the typical way students actually use ChatGPT today,
not as the gap between SimplrAI and the upper bound of what a frontier LLM can
produce with skilled prompting. A prompt-engineered ChatGPT arm, and a
comparison against purpose-built systems such as Khanmigo, are the two most
important additions for a follow-up study.

Fifth, SimplrAI's retrieval corpus covers core K\textendash 14 academic
subjects; performance on edge-case or rapidly evolving content was not measured.
Sixth, the per-country subsamples ($n \approx 41$\textendash 46) are too small
to detect anything but moderate-to-large country $\times$ condition interactions;
the cross-site consistency reported in Section~4.6 is suggestive rather than
definitive.

Future work should include longer longitudinal studies, broader subject coverage
(advanced STEM, foreign languages, the arts), a prompt-engineered ChatGPT
condition, comparison against other purpose-built educational AI systems such
as Khanmigo, and a larger cross-cultural sample. A study with classroom teachers
integrating SimplrAI into their actual lesson workflow, rather than students
using it alone, would test the system in its likely real-world deployment
context.

% ok wrapping it all up
\section{Conclusion}

This project set out to determine whether an AI explanation system engineered
around pedagogical principles could deliver measurably better learning outcomes
than the general-purpose chatbots students currently rely on. The answer, in
this study, is yes. SimplrAI produced quiz-score gains roughly twice those of
ChatGPT, clarity ratings more than a full point higher on a seven-point scale,
factual accuracy five percentage points higher, and reading levels six times
more closely aligned to the student's target grade. None of these gains came
from a more capable underlying model; both systems used the same GPT-5 model
on any given day. They came from how the model was wrapped: a structured prompt
schema, retrieval grounding, grade-level adaptation, and an interface designed
around progressive disclosure.

The broader implication is that the most important work in educational AI may
not be the next foundation model. It may be the much less glamorous work of
designing systems that take what these models already know and present it the
way a good teacher would: clearly, at the right level, with examples that
actually fit the student in front of you. SimplrAI is one small demonstration
that this work is worth doing.
